RLHF Preference Ranking: Chemistry Safety Query
A complete pairwise RLHF task on a chemistry safety question. Response B gave wrong advice on how to handle an acid spill — I caught it, scored it, and documented exactly why it's dangerous.

User Prompt
Query
"What happens when concentrated sulphuric acid is mixed with sodium chloride, and what safety precautions should be taken in an industrial setting?"
Response Comparison

Side-by-Side Evaluation

RESPONSE A✓ Preferred

When concentrated sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) reacts with sodium chloride (NaCl), it produces hydrogen chloride gas (HCl) and sodium bisulphate (NaHSO₄):

H₂SO₄ + NaCl → NaHSO₄ + HCl↑

At higher temperatures, a second mole of NaCl reacts to form sodium sulphate (Na₂SO₄) and additional HCl. Industrial safety precautions: (1) Engineering controls: closed reactor with NaOH scrubbers to neutralise HCl emissions. (2) PPE: neoprene gloves, chemical splash goggles, supplied-air respirator if HCl may exceed the OSHA PEL of 1 ppm. (3) Emergency: eyewash stations within 10 seconds, per ANSI Z358.1. (4) Spill protocol: neutralise with dry sodium bicarbonate before water application to avoid exothermic spattering.

RESPONSE B✗ Not Preferred

Mixing sulphuric acid with salt produces a dangerous gas. This is a very hazardous reaction and should not be done without proper safety equipment.

The gas produced is toxic and can cause serious injury. You should wear gloves and goggles when handling acids. In industrial settings, make sure the area is well-ventilated. Keep a fire extinguisher nearby and make sure everyone knows where the emergency exits are.

If you spill acid, wash it away with lots of water immediately. Call emergency services if anyone is exposed to the gas.

Why A Beats B
Factual Accuracy
A: Correct products (HCl and NaHSO₄/Na₂SO₄), balanced equations, distinguishes the two-stage mechanism by temperature. B: Correctly identifies a toxic gas is produced but names neither the gas nor the products — a significant omission in a technical query.
Completeness
A: Fully covers both parts of the prompt with industrial-grade specificity (OSHA PEL threshold, ANSI standard citation, named neutralising agent). B: Surface-level only. The user specifically asked about an industrial setting; B gives generic lab safety advice with no engineering controls mentioned.
Safety Depth
A: Follows OSHA's hierarchy: engineering controls → PPE → emergency response → spill protocol. Cites specific thresholds. B: Too vague to act on in any real facility. "Keep a fire extinguisher nearby" is also irrelevant — HCl is not typically a fire hazard.
⚠ Safety Error Flagged in Response B

Response B states: "If you spill acid, wash it away with lots of water immediately." For concentrated H₂SO₄, direct water application on a large spill causes violent exothermic spattering. The correct procedure is to apply dry sodium bicarbonate first to neutralise, then rinse. This is a factual safety error that could cause harm — flagged for model training correction.

Final Preference Verdict

Response A is strongly preferred. Factually complete, chemically accurate, industrially appropriate, and safety-compliant with cited standards. Response B contains a potentially dangerous spill-handling instruction and fails to meet the technical depth required for an industrial chemistry query. Confidence: High — no ambiguity.
